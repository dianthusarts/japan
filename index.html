Packing Context Sheet

The cultural and practical "why" behind what to bring — specific to Japan in early September

September reality check: Early September still feels like peak summer — hot, sticky, and occasionally very rainy. By the time you reach Kyoto in the second week, it cools slightly. Bring a compact umbrella always; Japan also sells excellent cheap ones at every convenience store. Typhoons are possible but typically pass in 1–2 days.

Temples & shrines

👟
Slip-on shoesYou'll remove shoes dozens of times — at ryokans, some temples, traditional restaurants. Laces are a burden.
🧦
Good socks, no holesYou will be sockless in public repeatedly. Japan notices.
🧣
Light scarf or shawlSome temples ask shoulders to be covered. Works as sun protection too.
🎒
Small daypackLockers at shrines and gardens are common; you'll store your main bag often.

Weather & comfort

👕
Lightweight, breathable fabricsLinen and moisture-wicking synthetics only. Cotton holds sweat. You'll walk 5+ miles most days.
☂️
Compact folding umbrellaBring one, but if you forget, 7-Eleven sells them for $5. Japan is extremely well-stocked.
🌡️
Light layer for indoorsJapanese air conditioning is aggressive. Restaurants and museums are often very cold.
🕶️
Sunglasses + SPF 50September sun is intense. Japanese sunscreen brands (Biore, Anessa) are excellent and cheap if you want to buy there.

Practical Japan-specifics

💴
Cash (¥30,000–¥50,000)Japan is still heavily cash-based. Many temples, small restaurants, and rural spots are cash-only. 7-Eleven and Japan Post ATMs accept foreign cards.
📶
Pocket wifi or SIMPre-order a pocket wifi rental or SIM card for delivery to your airport hotel. Google Maps in Japan is essential.
🔌
Type A plug (no adapter needed)Japan uses the same plugs as the US. Voltage is 100V — check your devices but most modern electronics are fine.
🎒
Luggage forwarding serviceJapan's "takuhaibin" will forward your big suitcase between hotels for ~$15. Pack a day bag so you can travel light on train days.

Ryokan night (essential)

🏯
Yukata is providedThe traditional robe is given to you at the inn. Don't pack a bathrobe.
🛁
Onsen etiquetteShower before entering the communal bath. No swimsuits — baths are used unclothed. Tattoos may be restricted at some onsens.
🧴
Minimal toiletriesRyokans provide high-quality shampoo, conditioner, face wash. Japanese drugstores (Matsumoto Kiyoshi) sell everything else cheaply.
📱
No shoes indoors — everAt your ryokan, never wear shoes past the entryway (genkan). Slippers are provided. Remove slippers before stepping on tatami mats.

Leave this at home

❌
Heavy guidebooksJapan Guide (japan-guide.com) on your phone is better than any paper guide.
❌
Large bottles of toiletriesBuy in Japan — cheaper, better quality, and fun to shop for.
❌
Revealing clothingShorts are fine for sightseeing, but very short skirts or low-cut tops are culturally out of place at temples.
❌
Perfume or strong scentsStrong fragrances are unusual in Japan and inappropriate in confined spaces like trains.

Small things that matter

📇
Business cards (meishi)If you have them, bring them. Exchanging cards is still a ritual in Japan. Receive cards with two hands, read them before putting away.
🎁
Small gifts from homeA local food item (maple candy, etc.) as a gift is deeply appreciated. Wrap it nicely — presentation is everything in Japan.
📓
Small notebookJapanese stationery is extraordinary. Buy one there — but bring a pen. Taking notes shows respect to guides.
😷
Face masksStill very common in Japan. Having a few is polite for crowded trains or if you feel unwell.

What You'll See at Each Stop

A quick preview of every destination on your itinerary — so nothing comes as a surprise

1
Tokyo
Days 2–4
  • Senso-ji Temple — Tokyo's oldest, in the ancient Asakusa district
  • Edo-Tokyo Museum — traces the city from shogunal capital to megacity
  • Imperial Palace East Gardens — the former innermost part of Edo Castle
  • Calligrapher's gallery visit (Koshun Masunaga)
Tokyo was called Edo until 1868. The tour is designed to show you both the ancient city and the modern one layered on top of it.
2
Mt. Fuji / Lake Suwa
Day 5
  • Mt. Fuji-Hakone-Izu National Park — the iconic volcano
  • Lake Ashi — views of Fuji across the water
  • Overnight in a ryokan (traditional inn)
  • Onsen (hot spring bath) experience
Fuji is a dormant volcano last active in 1707. It's been a spiritual site and artistic obsession for centuries — you'll recognize it from Hokusai's prints.
3
Takayama
Day 6
  • Old town Sanmachi Suji — preserved Edo-period merchant streets
  • Hida Folk Village — open-air museum of traditional farmhouses
  • Morning markets (if timing allows)
A mountain town that was a center of carpentry and craftsmen during the Edo period. Often called "Little Kyoto" — quieter and more rural than the famous cities.
4
Kanazawa
Days 7–8
  • Kenrokuen Garden — one of Japan's top 3 gardens (stroll garden, 11 hectares)
  • Higashi Chaya — historic geisha district, still active
  • Gold leaf craft gallery — 99% of Japan's gold leaf made here
  • Kanazawa Castle Park
Kanazawa survived WWII bombing because it had no military industry. Result: the most intact pre-modern city in Japan outside Kyoto.
5
Kyoto
Days 9–12
  • Nijo Castle — shogun's Kyoto residence; where the shogunate ended in 1867
  • Kinkaku-ji — the famous Golden Pavilion
  • Fushimi Inari Taisha — thousands of torii gates on a forested hill
  • Tea ceremony participation
  • Full day in Nara — giant Buddha, sacred deer, ancient capital
  • Arashiyama bamboo grove
Imperial capital for over 1,000 years. Deliberately spared from WWII bombing — which is why so much is intact. Four full days here is not enough.
H
Hiroshima + Miyajima
Extension
  • Peace Memorial Museum — the most important museum you'll ever visit
  • Atomic Bomb Dome — the only structure left standing near the epicenter
  • Peace Memorial Park and cenotaph
  • Miyajima Island — the "floating" torii gate of Itsukushima Shrine
  • Bullet train Kyoto → Hiroshima → Osaka
August 6, 1945. Today Hiroshima is one of Japan's most welcoming cities — its residents have made a conscious identity of peace advocacy. Come prepared emotionally.
